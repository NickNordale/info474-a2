Tool Description

For your visual layout, describe why it is an optimal choice for expressing your data effectively (~ 1 paragraph).
This tool serves two primary functions: it provides means to analyze the differences in statistical measures between teams and groups of teams, and also this tool can be used to analyze the statistical characteristics of good teams and bad teams. I chose a scatterplot for the visualization because scatterplots are a great tool to use in making comparisons and assessing relationships between variables. Humans are most capable of judging position against a common scale, which is exactly what a scatterplot provides. Also, scatterplots can facilitate interactive functionality in a way that is not visually overwhelming or confusing because it is a 2D plane and the axes positions remain constant.

Then, for each controlling element, detail how it enables your user to ask different questions (~1 paragraph).
I included two groups of controlling elements in this tool. One to toggle the conference of the NBA teams, and one to control which statistical metrics were displayed along the y-axis. The conference control, allows the user to analyze differences across either the whole population of teams, or differences across conferences. I added this because, for several years now, the Western Conference has widely been believed to be the superior conference. This control could lead to insights on why this discrepancy exists. The statistical metric control, allows the user to explore the relationships between various stats and winning percentage. More explicitly, the user could try and determine which stats are more likely to indicate a good team, and which stats might correlate strongly with losing teams/low win percentage.

You should include specific examples of insights you discovered through the use of these tools.
I discovered something interesting in the shooting percentage metrics (FG%, 3P%, FT%). It is intuitive that the teams who shoot better, would likely win more games. Such appears to be the case for FG% and 3P%, however, there seems to be virtually no correlation between FT% and WIN%. This is interesting both because you would expect better free throw shooting teams to win more because they earn more points when fouled, but this is also curious because it means that the winningest teams who are shooting high percentages from the field and from behind the three point line, are, relatively, shooting poorly at the line.
Also, in comparing the conferences, I noticed that the Eastern Conference points were bunched more closely together than the Western Conferences points for most of the metrics. This led to my hypothesis that, maybe the Eastern Conference contains teams which are somewhat similar to each other in style and makeup, whereas the Western Conference has a more diverse group of teams. This would take further analysis, but I could imagine that Eastern Conference teams could potentially have a harder time against each other because of how similar each team is to each other. This similarity and familiarity of style could decrease the variance of those matchups - ultimately normalizing the winning percentages of the top teams in that conference.

Key:
  • WIN%: Percentage of games won
  • FG%: Field goal percentage
  • 3P%: Three point percentage
  • FT%: Free throw percentage
  • PTS: Points per game
  • AST: Assists per game
  • REB: Rebounds per game
  • TOV: Turnovers per game
